Sora Shutdown: The Reality Check for AI Video & Governance in SMEs

Imagine you're planning your marketing strategy around a promising AI tool – and then it's unexpectedly discontinued. That's exactly what happened with OpenAI's highly anticipated text-to-video model, Sora, a real reality check for the fast-paced world of generative AI. What does this mean for Swiss SMEs that are already using AI or planning to, and why is solid AI governance more important than ever now?

Sora's Abrupt End: More Than Just an Isolated Case

On March 24, 2026, OpenAI announced that Sora – the groundbreaking model capable of generating hyper-realistic videos from text – would be discontinued. The app will be gone by April 26, 2026, with the API following on September 24, 2026. While this might initially seem like a setback for AI video technology, on closer inspection, it's an important lesson for all of us, especially for Swiss SMEs.

The reasons for the discontinuation are manifold and highlight the challenges even tech giants face: Firstly, there were the enormous operating costs – rumors suggest Sora was losing $15 million USD daily, while only generating $2.1 million USD through in-app purchases. This is a financial hole that even a company like OpenAI cannot plug indefinitely. Secondly, there was a strategic reorientation towards enterprise and coding tools, possibly in anticipation of an IPO in Q4 2026. Even a $1 billion partnership with Disney was terminated – Disney was reportedly informed only an hour before the public announcement. Imagine you're planning your campaigns with Disney characters, and the license is gone overnight.

This case clearly shows: the market for generative AI is extremely dynamic and unpredictable. New models like Sora 2, which was only released to selected users at the end of September 2025, can quickly disappear again. Competition from providers like Runway, Kling, Pika, and Google Veo 2 is immense. For you as an SME, this means: don't blindly rely on the hype. A solid strategy and the ability to react flexibly to such developments are crucial.

The Reality Check for Swiss SMEs: Between Hype and Hard Reality

The enthusiasm for AI in Swiss SMEs is undeniable. The proportion of companies using AI has risen from 22% in 2024 to 34% in 2026. This is a positive development, showing that you and your peers recognize the potential. But the Sora case is a cautionary tale that not every trend is sustainable, and not every tool celebrated as revolutionary today will still exist tomorrow.

While projects like the development of `strands-agents/sdk-python` in AI consulting for Swiss SMEs continue to show high relevance and activity, we must not ignore the underlying risks. The high computing costs for advanced AI videos are a central point that Sora painfully revealed. Your budget as an SME is limited – investments in AI must pay off and should not be sunk into a black box that suddenly disappears. While the funding of AI startups like Deccan AI, which completed a Series A round in March 2026, demonstrates confidence in the market, Sora's losses are a clear warning of financial volatility.

What does this mean for you? Carefully examine which AI tools you use. Are they from established providers? Is there a clear exit strategy if the tool is discontinued? How high are the ongoing costs, and are they sustainable? It's about finding a balance between the benefits of innovative AI and the need for stability and planning security in your daily business. The hype is tempting, but reality demands a sober assessment.

Legal Pitfalls and Reputational Risks: Your Responsibility in the AI Jungle

Sora's discontinuation not only exposed financial aspects but also reignited the discussion about copyright and the misuse of AI-generated content. As early as October 2025, Sora faced criticism for using copyrighted material by default. The Japanese Content Overseas Distribution Association urged OpenAI to cease using content from its members – including famous studios like Studio Ghibli. A week after the release of Sora 2 in September 2025, third-party programs for removing watermarks were already circulating, exacerbating the problem of unauthorized use and lack of labeling.

For you as a Swiss SME, this is a warning sign: if you use AI-generated videos or images for marketing, product development, or internal communication, you bear the responsibility for ensuring that this content is legally sound. This applies not only to copyrights but also to transparency towards your customers. The EU AI Act, whose transparency rules apply from August 2026, requires providers of generative AI to ensure that AI-generated content is identifiable and that deepfakes and texts of public interest are clearly labeled. Even if Switzerland is not (yet) directly part of the EU, these rules will become indirectly relevant if you collaborate with EU partners or offer products/services in the EU.

The reputational risk is enormous. Imagine your company being associated with a deepfake that damages your brand, or your marketing videos unknowingly containing copyrighted material. Such incidents can permanently destroy your customers' trust and lead to hefty fines. It is your responsibility to ensure that the AI tools you use are transparent and that you can always trace the origin and legality of the generated content.
