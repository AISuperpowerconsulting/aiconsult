8 out of 10 SMEs use AI plugins incorrectly: How to avoid the cost trap

The temptation is great: New AI plugins and models like DeepSeek V4 or GPT-5.5 promise massive efficiency gains. But the reality in Swiss SMEs often looks different: I see how 8 out of 10 companies use their AI tools incorrectly, not only wasting potential but also incurring unnecessary costs – often without even realizing it.

The Invisible Plugin Trap: More Tools, Less Output?

Everyone is talking about the latest AI models. DeepSeek V4, released as an open-source model in April 2026, offers a cost-effective alternative, while OpenAI's GPT-5.5 ('Spud'), with its System Card dated April 23, 2026, sets new performance benchmarks. The selection is vast, and many SMEs eagerly dive in, integrating a multitude of plugins and small AI tools into their workflows. The problem? Often, actions are impulsive rather than strategic. A new tool is installed because it sounds cool, but without clear use cases or integration into existing processes. The result is a patchwork of solutions that exist in isolation, don't complement each other, and ultimately create more confusion than value. The real challenge isn't the technology itself, but how intelligently it's managed.

Imagine you have a team of craftsmen. Each has the best specialized tool, but no one knows how to use it effectively in the overall project or how the tools interact. It's similar with many AI plugins in SMEs. They are introduced without employees developing the necessary skills to unlock their full potential. This leads to frustration, low adoption, and ultimately a negative ROI. It's not enough to simply have the latest models like DeepSeek V4 – you also need to know how to profitably integrate them into your specific business processes to truly benefit from their cost-effectiveness.

Data Privacy is Not a Luxury: The OpenAI Privacy Filter and Swiss Realities

In Switzerland, approaches to data privacy differ. While the EU AI Act is already in force in Europe, requiring Swiss companies exporting to the EU to meet its compliance requirements, Switzerland itself pursues a differentiated approach. The ratification of the Council of Europe's AI Convention and planned legislative drafts by the end of 2026 indicate that transparency, data protection, and oversight are becoming increasingly important in this country as well. This is precisely where the OpenAI Privacy Filter comes into play, with its development being crucial for 2026. It aims to ensure that sensitive data remains protected when using AI applications, particularly AI agents.

For Swiss SMEs working with AI experiments and agents, this is not an optional add-on but a necessity. Imagine using an AI plugin that processes customer data, and this data ends up unprotected on external servers. This is not only a reputational risk but can also lead to substantial fines. The adjustments to the Swiss Data Protection Act (DSG) require you to act proactively. This means you must know what data your plugins process, where it is stored, and how you ensure its protection. Without a clear plan and the necessary protective mechanisms, such as those the OpenAI Privacy Filter is intended to provide in the future, you are treading on thin ice. The integration of such filters is crucial for fostering innovation while simultaneously meeting the requirements of the DSG.

Skills, Not Just Clicks: Why Your Employees Are the Key

The best AI technology is useless if your employees don't know how to use it. Between 2024 and 2025, the adoption of AI tools in Swiss SMEs has indeed increased, but adoption is not the same as competence. I repeatedly observe companies investing heavily in licenses for AI tools but cutting corners on staff training. This is a classic misstep. Your employees need to know not only how to operate a plugin, but also *when* and *for what purpose* it is useful. It's about fostering critical thinking skills to question and refine AI outputs.

A concrete example: A marketing SME uses GPT-5.5 to generate blog articles. However, if employees lack knowledge of SEO, target audience analysis, or brand tone, the AI will produce generic texts that appeal to no one. The ability to write effective prompts, guide the AI, and critically evaluate its results is a core competency today. It's not about the AI taking over the work, but about employees leveraging AI as a powerful assistant. Invest in targeted training, internal workshops, and the development of 'AI agent leaders' within your team. These internal experts can then guide others and ensure that AI tools are not just used, but optimally deployed.

Mini AI Governance: ISO 42001 as a Compass for Plugins

For many SMEs, the term 'AI Governance' sounds like a huge, unattainable mountain. However, it is crucial, even if you only use a few plugins. Switzerland relies on a lean regulatory approach that promotes innovation while simultaneously managing risks. ISO 42001 for AI management systems, published in December 2023, offers an excellent framework, even if full certification is not necessary for most SMEs. You can use the principles of this standard to establish mini-governance for your AI plugins.

Specifically, this means: Define clear guidelines on which plugins may and may not be used within the company. Who is responsible for selection, implementation, and monitoring? What data may be processed? How do you ensure that the AI's outputs are fair, transparent, and traceable? A simple 'plugin register' where you record which tools you use, what they are used for, and which data protection regulations they comply with, is a first, important step. This not only helps you maintain an overview but also to meet the requirements of Swiss legislation and the Council of Europe Convention. It's about building trust in your AI usage – internally and externally.
